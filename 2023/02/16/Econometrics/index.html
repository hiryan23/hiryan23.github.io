Lecture 1: Introduction to Econometrics

Definition and Features of Econometrics

What's Econometrics?

  • Econometrics, literally "economic measurement", involves quantitative analysis of economic problems.
  • It is the application of statistical methods to connect theoretical economic models to data.
  • It is a bridge between abstract economic theory and real-world human economic activity.
  • Econometrics is the application of mathematics, statistical methods, and computer science, to economic data and is described as the branch of economics that aims to give empirical content to economic relations.
  • Empirical research using empirical evidence. Empirical evidence (the record of one's direct or indirect observations or experiences) can be analyzed quantitatively or qualitatively.
  • Can be used for:
    • Estimating economic relationships,
    • Testing economic theories,
    • Evaluationg and implementing policies

Features of Econometrics

Econometrics is an integration of economic theory and statistics.

Different from Pure Economic theory
  • Economic theory makes statements or hypotheses that are mostly qualitative in nature.
  • Econometrics provides numerical estimates, gives empirical content to most economic theory.
Different from Statistics
  • Statistics is mainly concerned with collecting, processing, presenting data in the form of charts and tables, and focuses on forecasting.
  • Econometrics: being concerned with using the collected data to test economic theories.
Different from Mathematical economics
  • Mathematical economics: Main concern is to express economic theory in mathematical form(equations) without regard to measurability or empirical verification of the theory.
  • Econometrics: Mainly interested in the empirical verification of economic theory. This conversion of mathematical into econometric equations requires a great deal of ingenuity and practical skill.

Usefulness and Importance of Econometrics

Comments of Econometrics

  • Hardness
  • Importance
    • In economics today, with the exception of pure theory, almost every academic article and government study uses econometrics of some kind.
    • Econometric skills play a special role in the training of economists.

Steps of Econometric Modelling

Methodology of econometrics

Traditional econometric methodology(empirical study) proceeds along the following lines:

  1. Statement of theory or hypothesis.
  2. Specification of the mathematical model of the theory.
  3. Specification of the statistical, or econometric model.
  4. Obtaining the data.
  5. Estimation of the parameters of the econometric model.
  6. Hypothesis testing.
  7. Forecasting or prediction.
  8. Using the model for control or policy.

Data Source

  • First hand data
    • Three Steps:
      1. Questionaire construction
      2. Sample Selection
      3. Data collection
  • Second hand data
    • Many macro and industrial data are available online or from yearbooks.
    • Data on individuals, households, and enterprises are usually difficult to obtain.

Data types

  • Cross section
  • Pure time series
  • Pooled data

Cross Sectional
  • Sample of individuals, households, firms, cities, states, countries, or other units of interest at a given point of time / in a given period
  • Cross-sectional observations are more or less independent
    • For example, pure random sampling from a population
  • Sometimes, pure random sampling is violated, e.g. units refuse to respond in surveys, or if sampling is characterized by clustering
  • Cross-sectional data typically encountered in applied microeconomics

Importance of causality

  • Causality and the notion of ceteris paribus
    • Definition of causal effect of \(x\) on \(y\) : "How does variable \(y\) change if variable \(x\) is changed but all other relevant factors are held constant"
  • Most economic questions are ceteris paribus questions
  • It is important to define which causal effect one is interested in
  • It is useful to describe how an experiment would have to be designed to infer the causal effect in question

Examples of Causal Effect

  • Causal effect of fertilizer on crop yield
    • "By how much will the production of soybeans increase if one increases the amount of fertilizer applied to the ground"
    • Implicit assumption: all other factors that influence crop yield such as quality of land, rainfall, presence of parasites etc. are held fixed
  • Experiment:
    • Choose several one-acre plots of land; randomly assign different amounts of fertilizer to the different plots; compare yields
    • Experiment works because amount of fertilizer applied is unrelated to other factors influencing crop yields

Lecture 2: Simple Linear Regression Model

Build a simple linear model

Capital asset pricing model (CAPM)

  • A model for pricing an individual security or portfolio. \[E(R_i)=R_f+\beta_i[E(R_m)-R_f]\]
  • \(E(R_i)\) is the expected return on the capital asset \(i\)
  • \(R_f\) is the risk-free rate of interest such as interest from government bonds
  • \(\beta\) is the sensitivity of the expected excess asset returns to the expected excess market returns
  • \(E(R_m)\) is the expected return of the market
  • We can build a simple linear regression model of \[R=\alpha +\beta R_m+u\]
  • \(R\) is the observed asset return on a specific asset
  • \(R_m\) is the market return, like S&P 500.
  • \(u\) is the error term.
How to estimate beta?
  • For any specific asset or portfolio, find enough observations at different time periods. (Shanghai Stock Exchange)
  • Then, we can draw the scatter plot of its return vs the market return. (SSE Index)

The Simple Regression Model

  • Definition of the simple linear regression model Explain variable \(y\) in terms of a single variable \(x\) . \[y=\beta_0+\beta_1x+u\]
    • \(y\) : Dependent variable, explained variable, regressand, left hand side variable, response variable, ...
    • \(\beta_0\) : Intercept, constant term
    • \(\beta_1\) : Slope parameter, coefficient
    • \(x\) : Independent variable, explanatory variable, right hand side variable control variable, regressor, covariate, ...
    • \(u\) : Error term, disturbance, unobservables, ...

Interpret the parameters

Interpretation

Interpretation of the simple linear regression model
  • Studies how \(y\) varies with changes in \(x\) . Ideally, we want to interprete it as \[\cfrac{\partial y}{\partial x}=\beta_1 \ \ \ \ \text{as long as} \ \ \ \ \cfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}=0\]
  • \(\cfrac{\partial y}{\partial x}=\beta_1\) : By how much does the dependent variable change if the independent variable is increased by one unit?
  • \(\cfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}=0\) : Interpretation only correct if all other things remain equal when the independent variable is increased by one unit (Infeasible!)

Causal Interpretation

When is there a causal interpretation?
Conditional mean independence assumption

\[E(u|x)=0\] The explanatory variable must not contain information about the mean of the unobserved factors

  • If this conditional mean assumption holds, then \[\begin{align} E(y|x)=E(\beta_0+\beta_1x+u|x)=\beta_0+\beta_1x\\ \beta_1=\cfrac{\text dE(y|x)}{\text dx} \end{align}\]
  • We study how the expectation of \(y\) varies with changes in \(x\) , which is measured by \(\beta_1\)
What does \(E(u|x)=c\) mean?
  • The most important math (statistic) concept in linear regression. (The teacher's point)
  • The key for the causal interpretation
  • Example: wage equation\[wage=\beta_0+\beta_1educ+u\]
  • In this example, \(E(u|x)=0\) requires \(E(intellegence|edu)=0\).
  • This means... ：条件期望为0是一个比不相关强，比独立弱的条件。
  • Compare:
    • \(E(u|X)=c\) vs \(E(u|X)=0\)
    • \(E(u)=c\) vs \(E(u|X)=c\)
    • \(E(u|X)=0\) vs \(\text{Cov}(u,X)=0\)
An illustration example (conditional vs unconditional mean)

  • Consider a college with half male and half female.

  • \(X\) is the gender, \(X=female\ \text{or}\ male\)

  • \(u\) is the height.

  • Suppose the average height is listed. | Gender | Height | |---|---| | female | 1.60m | | male | 1.72m |

  • \(E(u|X=female)=\)

  • \(E(u|X=male)=\)

  • \(E(u)=\)

  • \(E(u)\) is always a constant, not affected by \(X\) .

  • \(E(u|X)=c\) means that no matter what \(X\) is (male or female), the mean value of \(u\) is always the same.

The example continued

  • \(X\) is the school grade/year, \(X=1,2\)

  • \(u\) is the height. | Grade | College | Primary school | |---|---|---| | 1 | 1.66m | 1.15m | | 2 | 1.66m | 1.20m |

  • \(E(u|X=1)=E(u|X=2)=E(u)\) in college.

  • But not the same in the primary school. What happens if we change \(X\) back to gender?

Examples
  • Example: Crop yield and fertilizer \[yield=\beta_0+\beta_1fertilizer+u\]
    • \(\beta_1\) : Measures the effect of fertilizer on yield, holding all other factors fixed
    • \(u\) : Rainfall, land quality, presence of parasites, ...
  • Example: A simple wage equation \[wage=\beta_0+\beta_1educ+u\]
    • \(\beta_1\) : Measures the change in hourly wage given another year of education, holding all other factors fixed
    • \(u\) : Labor force experience, tenure with current employer, work ethic, intelligence, ...

Population Regression Function

Population regression function (PRF)
  • The conditional mean independence assumption implies that \[\begin{align} E(y|x)&=E(\beta_0+\beta_1x+u|x)\\ &=\beta_0+\beta_1x+E(u|x)\\ &=\beta_0+\beta_1x \end{align}\]
  • This means that the average value of the dependent variable can be expressed as a linear function of the explanatory variable
  • Formally, (compare to the ideal case) \[\beta_1=\cfrac{\text dE(y|x)}{\text dx}\ \ \ \ \text{and}\ \ \ \ \beta_0=E(y|x=0)\]

Derive the OLS estimator

A random sample

  • In order to estimate the regression model one needs data
  • A random sample of \(n\) observations \[\{(x_i,y_i):i=1,2,\cdots,n\} \begin{cases} (x_1,y_1)&\leftarrow \text{First observation}\\ (x_2,y_2)&\leftarrow \text{Second observation}\\ (x_3,y_3)&\leftarrow \text{Third observation}\\ \vdots\\ (x_n,y_n)&\leftarrow \text{n-th observation} \end{cases} \]
  • Fit as good as possible a regression line through the data points:

As good as possible

  • Regression residuals \[\hat u_i=y_I-\hat y_i=y_i-\hat\beta_0-\hat\beta_1x_i\]
  • Minimize sum of squared regression residuals \[\min\sum_{i=1}^n\hat u_i^2 \rightarrow \hat\beta_0,\hat\beta_1\]
  • Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) estimates \[\hat\beta_1=\cfrac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i-\bar x)^2},\ \ \ \ \hat\beta_0=\bar y-\hat\beta_1\bar x\]

Algebraic properties of the OLS estimator

Properties of OLS

  • Fitted values and residuals \[\hat y_i=\hat\beta_0+\hat\beta_1x_i \ \ \ \ \hat u_i=y_i-\hat y_i\]
    • \(\hat y_i\) : Fitted or predicted values
    • \(\hat u_i\) : Residuals (Deviations from regression line)
  • Difference between the error and the residual
  • Algebraic properties of OLS regression
    • \(\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n\hat u_i=0\) : Deviations from regression line sum up to zero
    • \(\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n\hat u_ix_i=0\) : Correlation between deviations and regressors is zero
    • \(\bar y=\hat\beta_0+\hat\beta_1\bar x\) : Sample averages of \(y\) and \(x\) lie on regression line

How well the model fit

  • Goodness-of-fit
    • How well does the explanatory variable explain the dependent variable?
  • Measures of variation
    • \(SST=\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n(y_i-\bar y)^2\) : Total sum of squares, represents total variation in dependent variable
    • \(SSE=\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n(\hat y_i-\bar y)^2\) : Explained sum of squares, represents variation explained by regression
    • \(SSR=\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n \bar u_i^2\) : Residual sum of squares, represents variation not explained by regression
  • Decomposition of total variation \[ SST=SSE+SSR\]
  • Goodness-of-fit measure (R-squared) \[R^2=\cfrac{SSE}{SST}=1-\cfrac{SSR}{SST}\]
  • R-squared measures the fraction of the total variation that is explained by the regression
  • A high R-squared does not necessarily mean that the regression has a causal interpretation!

Incorporating nonlinearities

  • Semi-logarithmic form \[\log y=\beta_0+\beta_1 x+u\]
    • The semi-log form assumes a semi-elasticity model.
  • log-logarithmic form \[\log y=\beta_0+\beta_1\log x+u\]
    • The log-log form postulates a constant elasticity model.

Statistic properties of the OLS estimator

Expected values and variances of the OLS estimators

  • The estimated regression coefficients are random variables because they are calculated from a random sample \[\hat\beta_1=\cfrac{\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i-\bar x)^2},\ \ \ \ \hat\beta_0=\bar y-\hat\beta_1\bar x\]
  • The question is what the estimators will estimate on average and how large their variability in repeated samples is \[E(\hat\beta_0)=?,\ \ \ \ E(\hat\beta_1)=?\ \ \ \ \text{Var}(\hat\beta_0)=?,\ \ \ \ \text{Var}(\hat\beta_1)=?\] #### Standard Assumptions
Assumption SLR.1 (Linear in parameters)

\[y=\beta_0+\beta_1 x+u\] - In the population, the relationship between \(y\) and \(x\) is linear

Assumption SLR.2 (Random sampling )

\[\{(x_i,y_i):i=1,\cdots,n\}\] - The data is a random sample drawn from the population \[y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1 x_i+u_i\] - Each data point therefore follows the population equation

Discussion of random sampling
  • Example: Wage and education
    • The population: all workers of country A
    • In the population, a linear relationship between wages (or log wages) and years of education holds
    • Draw completely randomly a worker from the population
    • The wage and years of education of the worker drawn are random because one does not know beforehand which worker is drawn
    • Throw back worker into population; repeat random draw \(n\) times
    • The wages and years of education of the sampled workers are used to estimate the linear relationship between wages and education
Assumption SLR.3 (Sample variation in explanatory variable)

\[\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i-\bar x)^2\textgreater0\] - The values of \(x\)'s are not all the same (otherwise it would be impossible to study how different values of \(x\) lead to different values of \(y\) )

Assumption SLR.4 (Zero conditional mean)

\[ E(u_i|x_i)=0\] - The value of the explanatory variable must contain no information about the mean of the unobserved factors

Unbiasedness of OLS
Theorem 2.1 (Unbiasedness of OLS)

\[ SLR.1-SLR.4\Rightarrow E(\hat\beta_0)=\beta_0,\ \ \ \ E(\hat\beta_1)=\beta_1\]

Interpretation of unbiasedness
  • The estimated coefficients may be smaller or larger, depending on the sample that is the result of a random draw
  • However, on average, they will be equal to the values that characterize the true relationship between \(y\) and \(x\) in the population
  • "On average" means if sampling was repeated, i.e. if drawing the random sample and doing the estimation was repeated many times
  • In a given sample, estimates may differ considerably from true values
Variances of the OLS estimators
  • Depending on the sample, the estimates will be nearer or farther away from the true population values
  • How far can we expect our estimates to be away from the true population values on average (=sampling variability)?
  • Sampling variability is measured by the estimator's variances
Assumption SLR.5 (Homoskedasticity(异方差性))

\[\text{Var}(u_i|x_i)=E(u_i^2|x_i)=\sigma^2\] - The value of the explanatory variable must contain no information about the variability of the unobserved factors

Graphical illustration of homoskedasticity

An illustrating example
  • \(X\) is the education, \(X=college\) or not.
  • \(u\) is the monthly wage.
  • Suppose there are only 4 people in the population with 2 having the college diploma. | wage | College | Non-college | |---|---|---| | | 10,000$ | 3000$ | | | 10,000$ | 17000$ | | E(wage|Edu) | 10,000$ | 10,000$ | | V(wage|Edu) | 0\(\$^2\) | 2*7000^2 \(\$^2\) |
  • Heteroskedasticity means that \(X\) does not affect the mean value of \(u\) , but does affect its variation.
Theorem 2.2 (Variances of OLS estimators)

Under assumptions SLR.1-SLR.5: \[\begin{align} \text{Var}(\hat\beta_1)&=\cfrac{\sigma^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i-\bar x)^2}=\cfrac{\sigma^2}{SST_x}\\ \text{Var}(\hat\beta_0)&=\cfrac{\sigma^2n^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^nx_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i-\bar x)^2}=\cfrac{\sigma^2n^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^nx_i^2}{SST_x} \end{align}\]

Conclusion:
  • The sampling variability of the estimated regression coefficients will be the higher the larger the variability to the unobserved factors, and the lower, the higher the variation in the explanatory variable
Unbiasedness of the error variance
Estimating the error variance
  • The variance of \(u\) does not depend on \(x\) , i.e. is equal to the unconditional variance \[\text{Var}(u_i|x_i)=\sigma^2=\text{Var}(u_i)\]
  • One could estimate the variance of the errors by calculating the variance of the residuals in the sample; unfortunately this estimate would be biased \[\tilde\sigma^2=\cfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n(\hat u_i-\bar{\hat u_i})^2=\cfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\hat u_i^2\]
  • An unbiased estimate of the error variance can be obtained by substracting the number of estimated regression coefficients from the number of observations \[\hat\sigma^2=\cfrac{1}{n-2}\sum_{i=1}^n\hat u_i^2\]
Theorem 2.3 (Unbiasedness of the error variance)

\[SLR.1-SLR.5\Rightarrow E(\hat\sigma^2)=\sigma^2\]

Calculation of standard errors for coefficients

\[\begin{align} se(\hat\beta_1)&=\sqrt{\widehat{\text{Var}}(\hat\beta_1)}=\sqrt{\hat\sigma^2/SST_x}\\ se(\hat\beta_0)&=\sqrt{\widehat{\text{Var}}(\hat\beta_0)}=\sqrt{\hat\sigma^2n^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^ns_i^2/SST_x} \end{align}\] - The estimated standard deviations of the regression coefficients are called "standard errors". They measure how precisely the regression coefficients are estimated.
